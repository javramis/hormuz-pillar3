The Mechanism

Fertilizer is made out of natural gas

Nitrogen fertilizer is built from natural gas — the gas literally becomes the fertilizer. The chain runs gas → ammonia → nitrogen fertilizer → crops → food, and most of the cost of nitrogen fertilizer is gas. That is why nitrogen — not the mined nutrients like potash — is how a Hormuz shock reaches the food system.

A Hormuz closure does not cut the Gulf off from gas. The Gulf has its own gas and makes fertilizer on site. What the closure does is trap that fertilizer behind the strait, so it cannot reach the countries that depend on it. India's problem is not that nitrogen cannot be made — it is that Gulf-made nitrogen cannot get out. It is a transit problem: the same logic as the oil and LNG in Pillars 1–2, one step downstream.

Layer 1 — Sourcing

Which producers does the world's food lean on?

Using the full bilateral nitrogen-trade network (UN Comtrade HS 3102, 2021–2024), we measure how much of each major agricultural importer's nitrogen comes from strait-region Gulf producers.

The Gulf sits at the core. India draws roughly 48% of its nitrogen from strait-region producers; Bangladesh, South Africa, and Thailand lean on them even more heavily. These are real, route-based dependencies — the supply lines a Hormuz closure would cut.

Layer 2 — Exposure

Exposure, mapped

Each country's strait-region-sourced share, scale-gated by absolute volume so that no-agriculture trade hubs don't dominate through tiny denominators — the same materiality logic used in Pillar 1.

A deliberate caveat on Oman. About 30% of India's nitrogen comes from Oman, whose ports face the Arabian Sea and so do not strictly transit the strait. We count Oman as conflict-region exposure — it holds Musandam, the strait's southern shore — but the headline depends on it: India is ~48% Gulf-sourced with Oman, ~17% on strictly in-strait producers. We flag this openly rather than bury it.

The Impact — Illustrative Scenario

Who actually loses food?

This layer is an illustrative scenario, not a prediction. Trade data shows exposure; it cannot forecast yield loss without agronomic modeling we don't have. The bounded what-if: if a closure removed strait-region nitrogen and that loss reached the field proportionally, what is the shape of who would be hit?

The answer is regressive. Because the nitrogen-yield response curve has diminishing returns (calibrated to the 150–200 kg N/ha sustainable range; Ludemann et al., Nature Food 2021), the same proportional cut barely dents a high-input country and devastates a low-input one. The most tonnage sits in large producers; the most devastating losses fall on low-input, strait-sourced countries least able to absorb them.

Volume: cereal tonnes at risk — large producers (India, US, Brazil) dominate.
Severity: % yield loss — low-input countries (Mozambique, Sudan, South Africa) hit hardest.

That contrast is the finding: the same shock, opposite geographies. The two channels below converge on the same victims.

Case Study

Jordan: hit on three fronts

Jordan surfaced in the combined index as Acute Dependence — not because it is large, but because it is structurally cornered. A continued closure hits it on three fronts at once, with no domestic buffer on any.

Jordan's exposure across the three pillars (higher = more exposed). Pillar 2 shown as substitution gap.
  • Energy — ~100% Gulf crude, near-zero self-sufficiency

    All of Jordan's crude routes through the Gulf; LNG supply is near-single-source (HHI 0.97); domestic energy production is essentially nil.

  • Substitution — little capacity to reroute

    Limited means to substitute away from strait-routed supply.

  • Food & fertilizer — import-dependent and a transmission node

    Jordan imports ~100% of its cereals, draws ~40% of its nitrogen from the Gulf, and re-exports nitrogen onward — chiefly to India. It is both exposed and a node in the regional food-security network.

That last role matters most for a food-systems lens: Jordan is not just an energy importer but a transmission point in the cascade — what happens to Jordan ripples onward to the countries it supplies.

Data & Methods

Open, reproducible, honest about its edges

Built on the same data spine and methodological grammar as Pillars 1–2: bilateral Comtrade flows, min-max normalization, a volume scale-gate ported from Pillar 1's materiality logic, and independence checks between axes.
